A 2-month-old bot with tetralogy of Fallot presented with poor activity and severe cyanosis. His heart rate was 40 beats per minute. The SpO2 was 40%.Which one is not correct?

(A) Give intravenous ketamine to treat this condition.

(B) Sodium bicarbonate infusion is required if severe metabolic acidosis.

(C) Knee-chest position is beneficial in the management of hypoxic spell.

(D) ** the heart murmur usually becomes louder during the attack of hypoxic spell

EXPLANATIONS BELOW

Concept note-1: -Infants with tetralogy of Fallot can have a bluish-looking skin color―called cyanosis―because their blood doesn’t carry enough oxygen. At birth, infants might not have blue-looking skin, but later might develop sudden episodes of bluish skin during crying or feeding. These episodes are called tet spells.

Concept note-2: -What is Cyanosis in Infants and Children? Cyanosis refers to a bluish-purple hue to the skin. It is most easily seen where the skin is thin, such as the lips, mouth, earlobes and fingernails. Cyanosis indicates there may be decreased oxygen attached to red blood cells in the bloodstream.

Concept note-3: -Tetralogy of Fallot is one of the commonest cyanotic heart defects. The severity of cyanosis is determined by the degree of obstruction to pulmonary blood flow. Prevention of cyanotic spells is key for safe anaesthesia. Late complications include arrhythmias, pulmonary regurgitation and right heart failure.

Concept note-4: -Tetralogy of Fallot is a combination of four congenital heart defects. The four defects are a ventricular septal defect (VSD), pulmonary stenosis, a misplaced aorta and a thickened right ventricular wall (right ventricular hypertrophy). They usually result in a lack of oxygen-rich blood reaching the body.
