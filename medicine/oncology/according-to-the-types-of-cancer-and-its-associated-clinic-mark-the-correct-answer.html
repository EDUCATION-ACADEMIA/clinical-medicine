EXPLANATIONS BELOW

Concept note-1: -Lung cancer and non-Hodgkin lymphoma (NHL) are responsible for 85% of malignancy-related SVCS cases. Small cell lung cancer (SCLC) and squamous cell carcinoma are the two most frequent lung cancer histologic types associated with SVCS.

Concept note-2: -Superior vena cava syndrome (SVCS) happens when the superior vena cava is partially blocked or compressed. Cancer is usually the main cause of SVCS.

Concept note-3: -SVC syndrome is more common in small cell lung cancer, occurring in approximately 10% of cases [6]. Other extrinsic etiologies of SVC syndrome include lymphoma and metastatic disease, as well as nonmalignant causes, including aortic aneurysm [7, 8].

Concept note-4: -The characteristic physical findings of SVCS include venous distention of the neck and chest wall, facial edema, upper-extremity edema, mental changes, plethora, cyanosis, papilledema, stupor, and even coma. Bending forward or lying down may aggravate the symptoms and signs.
